Lack of Exercise

With governments advising citizens to stay at home to prevent the risk of infection, there are less and less chances for students to go out for a walk or some exercise. Even with the chance to go exercise, the law forces us to have to wear masks outdoors to protect both others and our own health. Therefore, it is extremely hard to do any kind of exercise outdoors, especially more exhausting ones. The only solution left is to do exercise at home. But what can you do with limited space and equipment? Actually, there is so much you can do. Here is a sample workout plan that just uses a yoga mat and can be done in a few minutes just at home. No more excuses!

  • 20 Bodyweight squats.
  • 10 Push-ups.
  • 10 Walking lunges (each leg).
  • 10 Dumbbell rows (use a milk jug or other weight).
  • 15 Second Plank.
  • 30 Jumping jacks.

Inablilty to maintain a healthy diet

With more issues to address during the pandemic, who has time to buy ingredients and to cook a healthy meal? More and more people are ordering meals, which are likely not the best when it comes to a healthy diet. A healthy diet can help you prevent problems and chronic diseases such as obesity, diabetes, and heart disease. You might say, I don’t have time to prepare a healthy meal. But, it has never been easier to create healthy meals! Here are some meal plans that can be made within half an hour with some simple household ingredients.

Breakfast: natural (sugar-free) yoghurt with fresh fruits + boiled eggs and oatmeal yoghurt with fresh fruits + boiled eggs and oatmeal.

Lunch: boiled chicken breast and broccoli serve with lemon water (no sugar added)

Dinner: less-oil grilled salmon with boiled cabbage served with healthy pumpkin soup.

Not enough sunlight absorption

As mentioned above, it is harder and harder for students to go outdoors. A consequence of that is the lack of sunlight. Sunlight helps the human body to produce vitamin D, which is used to maintain calcium to prevent brittle, thin, or misshapen bones. A deficiency of vitamin D can lead to loss of bone density, which can contribute to osteoporosis and fractures (broken bones).

How do I get more vitamin D?

Of course, one simple solution is to try to be under the sunlight more often. It is recommended to get 8-15 minutes of exposure to sunlight every day. However, it is understood that it may be very difficult to get exposure to sunlight during the pandemic. Luckily, there are dietary sources of vitamin D. Some common sources include dark, fatty fish, fortified dairy and cereal products, mushrooms, and fortified orange juice.

Family relationship

During the pandemic, a lot of schools have switched to online classes. Therefore, students have to stare at a screen for a few hours a day continuously. When it comes to higher year levels, students may have to look at a monitor for over 5-8 hours. Another cause is that students are now forced to socialise with each other online in chat apps or social media. Excessive screen time not only strains your eyes and leaves them feeling dry, but can also lead to retina damage and blurred vision. Other problems include sleep deprivation, loss of cognitive ability and impaired socialising skills.

How do I control my screen time?

When using a screen for leisure, you can limit yourself to around 2 hours a day to prevent addiction. Also, around 30 minutes of screen time, you should allow your eyes to rest by looking at some greenery in the distance out the window or just by closing them. You can also do some stretching exercises to your eyes to relieve the strain. However, when it comes to online lessons, you can’t really control your screen time, or just rest your eyes in the middle of a lesson. Therefore, you must utilize the break time between lessons. During the breaks, relieve your eyes from looking at a screen and allow it to rest.
